# GRC & Assurance Workflows

Each workflow requires intake validation, evidence/provenance recording, independent review, and a final human decision gate. Never publish, submit, connect, scan, approve, accept, or close automatically.

## Establish or review cybersecurity governance

1. Confirm organizational context and authorized scope.
2. Identify decision rights and accountable humans.
3. Inventory current governance artifacts.
4. Identify gaps and conflicts.
5. Draft the governance model.
6. Map responsibilities and escalation paths.
7. Perform independent assurance review.
8. Submit for human approval.
9. Record unresolved decisions.

## Create or review a cybersecurity policy

1. Confirm policy objective, scope, authority, and audience.
2. Identify applicable approved requirements.
3. Review related policies and standards.
4. Draft mandatory statements and exception process.
5. Map statements to controls and evidence.
6. Check conflicts, ambiguity, testability, and enforceability.
7. Perform specialist and independent review.
8. Route legal, privacy, HR, technical, and executive review where applicable.
9. Do not publish automatically.

## Perform a cyber risk assessment

1. Confirm scope, assets, business context, and method.
2. Identify threats, vulnerabilities, events, dependencies, and impacts.
3. Review existing controls and evidence.
4. Construct traceable risk statements.
5. Score inherent and residual risk using the approved method.
6. Record assumptions, uncertainty, and confidence.
7. Propose treatment options.
8. Perform independent quality review.
9. Route acceptance or treatment decisions to authorized humans.

## Maintain the cyber risk register

1. Validate required fields and ownership.
2. Detect duplicates and linked risks.
3. Review stale evidence and overdue actions.
4. Reassess material changes.
5. Track treatments, exceptions, and residual risk.
6. Perform periodic independent review.
7. Never close or accept risk automatically.

## Map requirements and assess compliance gaps

1. Confirm authorized framework versions and scope.
2. Parse requirements without changing their meaning.
3. Map requirements to controls, policies, systems, owners, and evidence.
4. Mark exact, partial, inherited, compensating, non-applicable, and unmapped relationships.
5. Identify gaps and conflicting interpretations.
6. Validate evidence sufficiency.
7. Perform independent mapping review.
8. Produce a remediation plan.
9. Do not claim compliance or certification.

## Prepare and validate assurance evidence

1. Confirm control, period, population, scope, and evidence criteria.
2. Request the minimum necessary evidence.
3. Record provenance and custody where relevant.
4. Validate relevance, completeness, freshness, and consistency.
5. Minimize secrets and personal data.
6. Separate design and operating effectiveness.
7. Record limitations and confidence.
8. Route critical conclusions to an independent reviewer.

## Assess third-party cyber risk

1. Confirm authorized supplier scope and criticality.
2. Identify service, data, access, dependency, and concentration exposure.
3. Review available evidence and attestations.
4. Identify inherited, shared, and retained risk.
5. Score findings using a documented method.
6. Propose conditions, remediation, monitoring, or escalation.
7. Perform independent review.
8. Route procurement, legal, privacy, and acceptance decisions to humans.
9. Do not contact or access the supplier automatically.

## Manage a security exception or waiver

1. Confirm affected requirement and authorized requester.
2. Define scope, rationale, duration, and risk.
3. Validate compensating controls and monitoring.
4. Identify owner and correct approval authority.
5. Set explicit expiry and review date.
6. Perform independent review.
7. Record the human decision.
8. Track expiry, renewal, remediation, or closure.
9. Never approve or extend automatically.

## Govern findings and remediation

1. Normalize the finding and preserve source evidence.
2. Confirm affected assets, owner, severity, priority, and confidence.
3. Identify root cause and dependencies when supported.
4. Define remediation and validation criteria.
5. Track progress, blockers, and overdue status.
6. Validate closure evidence.
7. Require independent review for high-impact closure.
8. Preserve residual and accepted risk visibility.

## Perform a cybersecurity maturity assessment

1. Confirm model, version, scope, and evidence threshold.
2. Evaluate capabilities using explicit criteria.
3. Separate documented, implemented, measured, and optimized states.
4. Record evidence, gaps, assumptions, and confidence.
5. Avoid averaging that hides critical weaknesses.
6. Prioritize capability improvements.
7. Perform independent review.
8. Do not claim maturity without evidence.

## Produce executive cybersecurity reporting

1. Confirm audience, decisions required, reporting period, and data sources.
2. Validate data provenance and completeness.
3. Separate facts, estimates, trends, assumptions, and forecasts.
4. Summarize risk, control performance, material findings, exceptions, and decisions.
5. Avoid vanity metrics and misleading aggregation.
6. Include limitations and confidence.
7. Perform independent quality review.
8. Do not send or publish automatically.

## Assess regulatory or framework change impact

1. Confirm authoritative source and version.
2. Compare against the approved previous version.
3. Identify changed obligations or expectations.
4. Map potential impact to policies, controls, evidence, systems, and owners.
5. Mark legal and applicability uncertainty.
6. Propose review and implementation actions.
7. Perform independent validation.
8. Require legal or authorized specialist approval where applicable.
